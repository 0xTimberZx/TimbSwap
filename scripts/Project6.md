PoolTogether is probably the closest real-world comparison to your idea, but there are some fundamental differences. One thing to note: the file you uploaded is the developer documentation README, not the protocol design itself, so it doesn't describe the economics or mechanics of the game.

That said, here's how I'd evaluate PoolTogether as inspiration.

What PoolTogether gets right
1. No principal loss

This is its biggest innovation.

Players deposit money into a prize pool. Their deposit isn't lost—it remains theirs to withdraw. Instead, the protocol generates yield (originally through lending protocols), and that yield becomes the prize.

This removes one of gambling's biggest psychological barriers.

Result: Much easier to trust.

2. Positive-sum economics

Unlike casinos:

Users don't lose deposits.
Winners receive generated yield.
Everyone keeps ownership of their principal.

It's one of the few crypto games where players don't collectively become poorer over time.

3. Simple value proposition

People understand it immediately:

"Save money. Maybe win a jackpot."

No complicated tokenomics required.

4. Sustainable rewards

Rewards come from:

lending yield
protocol incentives
sponsorships

—not from minting infinite tokens.

That avoids many GameFi failures.

Weaknesses
1. Boring

This is probably its biggest weakness.

After depositing...

...you wait.

There is almost zero gameplay.

No strategy.

No excitement between drawings.

2. Dependent on yield

If ETH staking yields drop...

Prize pools shrink.

During low-rate markets PoolTogether becomes much less attractive.

3. Capital inefficient

Millions of dollars may sit idle just to generate relatively small yields.

Large TVL is required for exciting prizes.

4. Weak viral loop

People rarely tell friends

"Come watch my savings account."

Compare that with poker...

or fantasy sports...

or prediction markets...

Those naturally generate stories.

Where your idea is stronger

Your proposal introduces active gameplay.

Instead of:

Deposit → Wait → Draw

you have something closer to:

Deposit → Play → Win → Repeat

That creates engagement instead of passive waiting.

Where your idea is weaker

Your version introduces principal risk.

Players can lose ETH.

That means:

harder legal questions
more emotional losses
higher trust requirements
potentially fewer casual users

PoolTogether avoids almost all of those issues because deposits remain redeemable.

Other games worth studying
1. Axie Infinity

Strengths

Incredible player growth
Strong NFT economy
Community

Weaknesses

Inflation
Needed constant new players
Reward token collapsed

Lesson:

Never pay rewards from endless emissions.

2. Zed Run

Digital horse racing.

Strengths

Collectible assets
Breeding economy

Weaknesses

Expensive entry
Liquidity dried up

Lesson:

Scarcity alone doesn't create demand.

3. Rollbit

One of crypto's biggest gambling platforms.

Strengths

Revenue-driven
Token buybacks
Real cash flow

Weaknesses

Gambling regulation
Whale dependence

Lesson:

Revenue-backed tokens survive much longer than reward tokens.

4. Blast's Gold points

Strengths

Incentivized usage

Weaknesses

Speculation dominated utility

Lesson:

Points are great for bootstrapping, but they shouldn't become the entire economy.

5. Friend.tech

Strengths

Viral launch
Novel mechanics

Weaknesses

No lasting utility
Activity collapsed

Lesson:

Novelty isn't enough.

What I think your biggest opportunity is

I don't actually think you're competing with PoolTogether.

I think you're sitting somewhere between:

PoolTogether (capital preservation)
Poker (player skill and competition)
Lottery (large jackpots)
On-chain games (transparent settlement)

That hybrid could be genuinely interesting if designed carefully.

The biggest question I'd encourage you to answer is:

Why does someone come back after their first game?

If the answer is only "to win more ETH," you'll end up competing with casinos.

If the answer is "because the game itself is genuinely fun, competitive, and social," then you've built something much harder to copy and potentially much more durable.

From everything we've discussed so far, I think your biggest challenge is no longer the tokenomics—it's designing a gameplay loop that's engaging enough that players would return even if the token price were flat. That's often the difference between a sustainable game and a financial product wrapped in game mechanics.
